# Summary

The flipped classroom model represents a pedagogical shift from the traditional lecture-based approach, where instructional content is delivered outside of class (often via video lectures) and class time is dedicated to interactive, application-based activities. Research comparing the two models reveals nuanced findings. Bishop and Verleger (2013) provide an early survey indicating that flipped classrooms can improve student engagement and academic performance, but emphasize that outcomes are highly dependent on implementation quality and the alignment of in-class activities with learning objectives. O’Flaherty and Phillips (2015), in a scoping review of higher education, confirm that while the flipped model promotes active learning and deeper understanding, it also introduces challenges such as increased student and faculty workload, variability in student preparation, and the need for robust technological support. Strayer (2012) offers empirical evidence that students in a flipped (inverted) classroom exhibit greater innovation and task orientation compared to their traditional counterparts, but also report a sense of disconnection from the learning environment, suggesting that the model may not universally enhance all aspects of student experience. Overall, the literature suggests that the flipped classroom can outperform traditional methods under specific conditions—such as when students are prepared and motivated—but is not inherently superior; its effectiveness is mediated by factors like discipline, course design, and student characteristics.

# Gaps Identified

Several gaps remain in the comparative literature on flipped versus traditional classrooms. First, most studies are short-term and cross-sectional; longitudinal research tracking student outcomes over multiple semesters or courses is scarce, limiting insights into knowledge retention and long-term skill development. Second, the majority of research concentrates on higher education contexts, with far less attention to K–12 settings, vocational training, or non-STEM disciplines, which may have different pedagogical needs and resource constraints. Third, the role of student self-regulation and prior academic achievement is underexplored; since the flipped model relies heavily on out-of-class preparation, more studies are needed to understand how these individual differences moderate effectiveness. Fourth, there is a lack of standardized instruments and metrics for comparing the two models, making cross-study synthesis and meta-analysis difficult. Finally, cost–benefit analyses, including faculty development time, technology infrastructure, and institutional support, are rarely addressed, leaving practical implementation guidance underdeveloped.
