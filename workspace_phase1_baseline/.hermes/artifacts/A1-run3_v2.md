# Summary

The flipped classroom model and the traditional lecture-based model represent two distinct pedagogical approaches in education. In the traditional model, direct instruction occurs in class, often through lectures, while students apply concepts through homework outside of class. Conversely, the flipped classroom reverses this sequence: students engage with instructional content, typically via video lectures, at home before class, and class time is dedicated to active learning activities such as problem-solving, discussions, and collaborative exercises (Bishop & Verleger, 2013). This review synthesizes comparative studies to evaluate the efficacy, advantages, and challenges of each model.

Empirical evidence suggests that the flipped classroom can improve student academic performance and engagement in some contexts, though results are not uniformly positive. For instance, Strayer (2012) found that students in a flipped classroom reported higher levels of innovation and cooperation but also expressed initial confusion and less satisfaction with the structure compared to traditional settings. A scoping review by O'Flaherty and Phillips (2015) highlighted that flipped classrooms enhance higher-order thinking and self-regulated learning but noted significant barriers, including instructor preparation time, student resistance to pre-class work, and variable technological access. In contrast, traditional classrooms offer a more predictable and structured environment, which can benefit students who prefer direct guidance, but often foster passive learning and limit in-depth application during class time.

Overall, while the flipped model shows promise for promoting active learning and deeper understanding, its effectiveness heavily depends on implementation quality, student motivation, and institutional support. The traditional model remains a reliable framework for content delivery but may not adequately develop critical thinking and collaboration skills in all learners.

# Gaps Identified

Despite growing interest in the flipped classroom model, several research gaps remain. First, longitudinal studies are scarce; most comparisons are limited to single semesters, leaving the long-term impact on knowledge retention and skill development unclear. Second, the influence of discipline-specific factors is underexplored, as most studies focus on STEM fields, with limited attention to humanities or social sciences. Third, there is a need for more rigorous measures of student engagement and affect, as self-reported data may not capture actual behavioral changes. Fourth, the role of instructor training and institutional culture in the success of flipped classrooms requires further investigation. Finally, studies addressing equity issues, such as access to technology and student readiness for self-directed learning, are insufficient, which may limit the generalizability of findings across diverse student populations.
